Overall Summary

Overview

Creatine supplementation increased total sleep duration on resistance training days in naturally menstruating women compared to placebo. No significant changes were observed in chronic sleep quality (PSQI), strength gains, or body composition. Both groups experienced a decrease in caloric, protein, and fat intake during the study. The primary finding of increased total sleep duration on training days was specific to the creatine group and suggests a potential benefit for post-exercise recovery.

Section: Conclusions

Conclusion

This study presents moderate evidence for the positive effect of creatine supplementation on acute total sleep duration following resistance training in naturally menstruating women. The randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled design is a significant strength, minimizing bias and supporting causal inferences. The use of objective sleep measures (Oura ring) and robust statistical analysis (MLM) further strengthens the evidence. However, the study's small sample size, the lack of reported effect sizes, the unexplained dietary changes during the intervention, and the limited 6-week timeframe somewhat limit the generalizability and long-term implications of the findings. While the study effectively demonstrates an acute effect on sleep duration on training days, the lack of significant changes in chronic sleep quality or strength gains warrants further investigation. Overall, the study makes a valuable contribution to the field by exploring the effects of creatine on sleep in a relatively understudied population, but further research is needed to confirm and expand upon these findings, particularly regarding long-term effects, optimal supplementation durations, and the interplay between creatine, sleep, and dietary intake.

Section Analysis

Abstract

Key Aspects

  • Study Rationale and Focus: This study investigated the impact of creatine supplementation on sleep in naturally menstruating women, a demographic often underrepresented in this area of research. The rationale stems from observations that women generally experience poorer sleep quality and have lower creatine stores compared to men. Creatine's potential role in mitigating sleep disturbances, particularly those linked to demanding physical activity, formed the core of the research question. This focus is relevant due to the known interplay between sleep, exercise, and overall well-being, especially in women whose hormonal fluctuations can further influence sleep patterns.
  • Experimental Design and Methodology: The study employed a double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled design, considered the gold standard in clinical research for minimizing bias and ensuring reliable results. Twenty-one women participated, consuming either creatine or a placebo daily for six weeks. All participants engaged in twice-weekly resistance training using a TONAL device, a wall-mounted electromagnetic resistance system. This standardized exercise regimen allowed researchers to isolate the effects of creatine supplementation on sleep while controlling for the influence of physical activity.
  • Data Collection and Measurements: Sleep was assessed nightly using an Oura ring, a wearable device that tracks various sleep parameters. This provided continuous, real-world data on sleep duration and quality, unlike single-point measurements that might not capture the full picture of sleep patterns over time. Additionally, body composition, dietary intake, and muscular strength were assessed before and after the intervention to monitor potential creatine-related changes beyond sleep. These secondary measures helped to create a more holistic profile of the participants' responses to creatine supplementation and resistance training.
  • Key Findings and Outcomes: The primary finding was a significant increase in total sleep duration on resistance training days for the creatine group compared to the placebo group. This suggests that creatine may enhance sleep recovery after strenuous exercise. However, no significant changes were observed in overall chronic sleep quality or in measures like the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI). While both groups improved in muscular strength, there were no significant differences between them, indicating that creatine didn't provide an additional boost to strength gains in this specific context.
  • Implications and Future Research: The study's results contribute to the growing body of knowledge on creatine's potential benefits beyond muscle performance. The observed improvement in sleep duration following resistance training suggests a possible role for creatine in enhancing recovery and mitigating sleep disturbances, particularly in women. However, the lack of overall improvement in chronic sleep quality warrants further investigation into the long-term effects of creatine on sleep patterns. The study also highlights the importance of using objective sleep measures like the Oura ring for capturing real-world sleep data in exercise interventions.

Materials and Methods

Key Aspects

  • Study Design and Participant Characteristics: This study employed a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled design, the gold standard for minimizing bias and isolating the effects of an intervention. Twenty-seven naturally menstruating, non-resistance-trained females (18-24 years old) were initially enrolled. Participants were matched based on body weight, physical activity, and animal protein consumption before being randomly assigned to either the creatine (CR) or placebo (PLA) group. All participants engaged in a standardized 6-week resistance training program, ensuring that any observed differences between groups could be attributed to the creatine supplementation. This rigorous design enhances the internal validity of the study, allowing for stronger causal inferences.
  • Supplementation Protocol: The study used Creapure® creatine monohydrate and a corn-starch maltodextrin placebo. Participants consumed their assigned supplement daily, Monday through Friday, in the lab, and were given weekend doses to take home. Compliance was monitored via photo and video evidence. The creatine group received 5g of creatine mixed with 5g of maltodextrin, while the placebo group received 10g of maltodextrin. This ensured both groups consumed the same total amount of powder, maintaining blinding and controlling for potential effects of maltodextrin itself. The detailed protocol and compliance monitoring strengthen the study's methodological rigor.
  • Resistance Training Intervention: The resistance training program involved twice-weekly sessions using a TONAL® device, a wall-mounted electromagnetic resistance system. The program lasted 6 weeks and included both beginner and intermediate full-body workouts, supervised by a researcher. While the specific exercises, sets, and repetitions are not detailed, the use of a standardized program and supervised sessions aimed to control for exercise variations and ensure participant safety. This approach helps isolate the effects of creatine on sleep, independent of training differences.
  • Sleep Assessment: Sleep was assessed daily using an Oura ring, a wearable device that tracks sleep quantity and quality metrics like total sleep time, sleep efficiency, and time spent in different sleep stages. This continuous, objective monitoring provides a more comprehensive view of sleep patterns compared to single-point assessments. Additionally, the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI), a subjective measure of sleep quality, was administered pre- and post-intervention. Using both objective and subjective measures allows for a more nuanced understanding of sleep changes.
  • Body Composition Assessment: Body composition was assessed using dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA) and bioelectrical impedance analysis (BIA). DXA, considered a gold standard for body composition measurement, provides precise estimates of fat mass, lean mass, and bone density. BIA, a quicker and less expensive method, estimates body water compartments, which can be used to infer body composition. These measurements were taken pre- and post-intervention to assess the impact of creatine on body composition changes alongside the resistance training.
  • Dietary Intake Assessment: Dietary intake was assessed using the Diet History Questionnaire III (DHQ III), a comprehensive online tool that captures information about food and supplement consumption over the past 30 days. This allowed researchers to estimate habitual creatine intake from dietary sources and monitor any changes in dietary patterns during the intervention. This information is crucial for understanding the nutritional context of the study and controlling for potential confounding effects of diet on sleep and body composition.
  • Muscular Strength Assessment: Muscular strength was assessed using the TONAL® Strength Assessment, a protocol based on a 3-repetition maximum lift for four primary movements: bench press, neutral grip deadlift, seated lat pulldown, and seated shoulder press. This assessment provided a baseline measure of strength and allowed for tracking changes in strength over the course of the intervention. Using the same device for both training and assessment ensures consistency and allows for direct comparison of strength gains.
  • Statistical Analysis: Statistical analyses were performed using SPSS and jamovi software. Repeated-measures ANOVAs were used to analyze pre- and post-intervention data, while multilevel modeling (MLM) was employed to analyze daily sleep data, accounting for the nested structure of the data and handling missing values. This approach allows for a more robust analysis of changes over time and considers the individual variability in sleep patterns.

Results

Key Aspects

  • Increased Sleep Duration on Training Days with Creatine: The primary finding of this study was an increase in total sleep duration on resistance training days for the creatine group compared to the placebo group. This key result suggests that creatine supplementation may enhance sleep recovery after exercise. The increase in sleep duration was specific to training days, with no significant differences observed on non-training days. This targeted effect highlights the potential role of creatine in mitigating exercise-induced sleep disturbances. Think of it like this: if exercise creates a 'sleep debt', creatine might help 'pay it off' more efficiently on the nights following training.
  • No Change in Chronic Sleep Quality: While the creatine group experienced increased sleep duration on training days, there were no significant changes in chronic sleep quality for either group, as measured by the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI). This suggests that while creatine may improve sleep quantity after exercise, it may not have a substantial impact on overall sleep quality over the 6-week intervention period. It's like adding an extra hour of sleep after a tiring workout, but not necessarily changing how restful that sleep feels overall.
  • Similar Strength Gains in Both Groups: Both the creatine and placebo groups showed improvements in muscular strength, as measured by the TONAL Strength Assessment. However, there were no significant differences between the groups in terms of strength gains. This indicates that in this specific context, creatine supplementation did not provide an additional boost to strength development beyond the effects of resistance training itself. Imagine two groups climbing a hill, both making progress, but neither significantly outpacing the other.
  • Dietary Changes During the Intervention: Both groups experienced a reduction in total calorie intake, protein intake (both absolute and relative to body weight), fat intake, and relative carbohydrate intake. These dietary changes, while not the primary focus of the study, are important to note as they could potentially influence the interpretation of the results. Changes in diet, especially reductions in calorie and macronutrient intake, can affect sleep patterns and body composition, potentially confounding the effects of creatine supplementation and exercise.
