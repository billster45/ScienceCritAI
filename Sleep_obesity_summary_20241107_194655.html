Overall Summary

Overview

Insufficient sleep increases 24-hour energy expenditure by 4-5% (approximately 100 calories/day in young adults) but often leads to increased food intake and weight gain under ad libitum conditions. Circadian misalignment decreases 24-hour energy expenditure and alters appetite hormones (ghrelin, leptin, PYY), potentially promoting weight gain. Each hour of social jetlag increases the odds of metabolic syndrome by 30%.

Conclusion

The review presents a compelling case for the importance of sleep and circadian rhythms in metabolic health, synthesizing a substantial body of evidence from human studies. The overall quality of evidence is high, particularly in areas where controlled laboratory studies have been conducted, such as the impact of insufficient sleep on energy expenditure and appetite hormones. The review effectively addresses its research question by demonstrating a clear link between sleep disruption (both insufficient sleep and circadian misalignment) and increased obesity risk. The methodological rigor of the included studies is generally strong, with frequent use of gold-standard techniques like the constant routine protocol and whole-room calorimetry. However, the review also acknowledges the limitations of relying heavily on cross-sectional studies for investigating social jetlag and the impact of obesity on sleep, calling for more longitudinal research in these areas. The findings are significant and reliable for the field, providing valuable insights into the complex interplay between sleep, circadian rhythms, and metabolic health. The review's focus on human studies strengthens the translatability of the findings to clinical practice and public health interventions. The discussion of potential strategies to improve sleep, while comprehensive, could benefit from more specific and actionable recommendations. Overall, the review makes a valuable contribution to the field by highlighting the importance of sleep and circadian health in the context of the obesity pandemic and providing a strong foundation for future research and interventions.

Energy expenditure and appetite hormones

Key Aspects

  • Energy Expenditure: Energy expenditure, the amount of energy the body uses, is influenced by both sleep and circadian rhythms. It's like a furnace: it burns hotter when we're awake and cooler when we're asleep. This variation is driven partly by our internal clock, which sets a daily rhythm, and partly by the sleep-wake cycle itself. When sleep is disrupted, this rhythm gets thrown off, potentially affecting overall energy balance.
  • Appetite Hormones: Ghrelin, leptin, and PYY are key hormones that regulate appetite, acting as signals between the gut and the brain. Ghrelin is like the 'hunger hormone,' increasing appetite, while leptin and PYY act like 'satiety hormones,' decreasing appetite. These hormones also follow a daily rhythm, influenced by both the circadian clock and sleep-wake cycles. Insufficient sleep and circadian misalignment can disrupt these hormonal patterns, potentially leading to increased hunger and altered food intake.
  • Constant Routine Protocol: The constant routine protocol is a gold standard method for studying circadian rhythms. It involves keeping participants in carefully controlled conditions (dim light, constant temperature, regular small meals or continuous feeding) to isolate the effects of the internal clock from external influences. This method helps researchers understand how much of a physiological process, like hormone release or energy expenditure, is truly driven by the circadian clock versus other factors.
  • Impact of Insufficient Sleep: Insufficient sleep increases 24-hour energy expenditure, but it also increases food intake, often leading to a net positive energy balance and weight gain. It's like a car with a gas leak: the engine runs faster (higher expenditure), but you have to fill the tank more often (increased intake), ultimately using more fuel overall. This increased intake seems to be driven more by changes in brain reward pathways and cognitive control than by appetite hormones alone.
  • Impact of Circadian Misalignment: Circadian misalignment, such as occurs in shift work, reduces 24-hour energy expenditure and alters appetite hormone levels, potentially promoting weight gain. It's like having your internal clock set to the wrong time zone: your body's signals for hunger and energy use are mismatched with your actual activities and environment. This misalignment can also lead to less healthy food choices and reduced physical activity, further contributing to metabolic problems.

First Reference in Text

Description
  • Overview of Figure Panels: Figure 1 shows how energy expenditure changes over a 24-hour period under different sleep and circadian conditions. Energy expenditure is the amount of energy the body uses, often measured in kilojoules (kJ). The x-axes typically represent time, either as hours from scheduled wake time, or as "relative clock hour" linked to the individual's melatonin onset. Melatonin is a hormone that regulates sleep, and its onset is used as a marker of the biological night. The y-axes represent energy expenditure, either in absolute units (kJ/min) or as a percentage change from a baseline. Panel (a) compares energy expenditure during a normal sleep schedule (16 hours awake, 8 hours of sleep) with a period of total sleep deprivation (24 hours awake). Panel (b) shows the inherent circadian rhythm of energy expenditure under constant conditions, meaning factors like light, food intake, and posture are carefully controlled to isolate the effect of the body's internal clock. Panel (c) shows energy expenditure during adequate and insufficient sleep, with or without controlling food intake. Panel (d) illustrates the impact of simulated night shift work (sleeping during the day) on energy expenditure.
  • Constant Routine Protocol: A constant routine protocol is a research method used in chronobiology to isolate the effects of the body's internal clock (the circadian rhythm) on physiological processes. Participants are kept in carefully controlled conditions, such as dim light, constant temperature, and regular small meals or continuous feeding, to minimize the influence of external factors. This method helps researchers identify the true circadian rhythm of a process, like energy expenditure, independent of other influences like sleep or food intake.
  • Thermic Effect of Food: The thermic effect of food, also known as diet-induced thermogenesis, refers to the increase in energy expenditure that occurs after eating a meal. It represents the energy the body uses to digest, absorb, and process the nutrients from the food.
  • Whole-Room Calorimeter: A whole-room calorimeter is a specialized chamber used to measure a person's energy expenditure. It precisely tracks oxygen consumption and carbon dioxide production, which are used to calculate energy expenditure. This method is highly accurate and sensitive compared to other methods like doubly labelled water.
  • Doubly Labelled Water: Doubly labelled water is another method used to estimate energy expenditure over longer periods, typically days or weeks. It involves giving a person water containing isotopes (slightly different forms) of hydrogen and oxygen. By measuring how these isotopes are eliminated from the body, researchers can estimate energy expenditure. This method is less precise than calorimetry but is more practical for free-living studies.

Impact of insufficient sleep

Key Aspects

  • Increased Energy Expenditure: Insufficient sleep, much like total sleep deprivation, leads to a noticeable increase in 24-hour energy expenditure. Think of it like your body's engine revving slightly higher because it's awake longer. This increase, about 4-5% or roughly 100 calories per day in young adults, is measurable using precise methods like whole-room calorimetry. Interestingly, this increase happens regardless of whether food intake is kept constant or allowed to vary freely. However, less sensitive techniques like doubly labeled water might not pick up on these subtle but still important changes.
  • Controlled Energy Intake and Increased Hunger: When food intake is strictly controlled to match the needs of a well-rested day, insufficient sleep creates a negative energy balance. This means the body burns more calories than it consumes, like a car running on fumes. This energy deficit, coupled with hormonal shifts like increased ghrelin (the hunger hormone) and decreased leptin (the satiety hormone), leads to a predictable increase in hunger. It's as if your body is sending out an SOS for more fuel. Interestingly, PYY, another hormone that suppresses appetite, hasn't been studied much in this specific scenario of controlled food intake during insufficient sleep.
  • Uncontrolled Energy Intake and Weight Gain: In real-world scenarios where food is readily available (ad libitum), insufficient sleep triggers a calorie consumption surge that outpaces the increased energy expenditure. It's like having a bigger engine but also a much larger gas tank. This leads to a positive energy balance, meaning more calories are stored than burned, and ultimately weight gain. Surprisingly, this overeating occurs despite hormonal changes that should signal fullness, like decreased ghrelin, increased leptin, and increased PYY. This suggests that factors beyond these hormones, such as changes in brain reward centers or impulse control, are driving the increased food intake.
  • Mixed Effects on Gut Hormones: Studies on how insufficient sleep affects gut hormones like GLP-1 and pancreatic polypeptide, which play roles in satiety and glucose regulation, have yielded mixed results. These hormones are like fine-tuning knobs in the digestive system, and it seems sleep disruption might tweak them differently depending on factors like sex and individual circumstances. For instance, some studies show decreased afternoon GLP-1 levels in sleep-restricted women but not men, while others find similar decreases in men experiencing fragmented sleep. Similarly, pancreatic polypeptide levels don't always change consistently with sleep restriction. These inconsistencies highlight the complexity of the gut-brain connection in the context of sleep.

  • Treatment for Sleep Disorders: Effective treatment for sleep disorders and related mental health conditions can improve overall health outcomes. Addressing emotional stress and mental health issues is crucial, as they are significant risk factors for insufficient sleep and obesity. It's like fixing the underlying problems - treating sleep disorders and mental health issues addresses the root causes of poor sleep and metabolic dysfunction, leading to better overall health.

Conclusions

Key Aspects

  • Insufficient Sleep and Energy Imbalance: Insufficient sleep disrupts the delicate balance of energy regulation. It leads to increased 24-hour energy expenditure, as the body's metabolic engine runs hotter for longer. However, under conditions of free access to food, this increased expenditure is overshadowed by an even greater surge in calorie intake, driven by changes in appetite hormones and reward pathways in the brain. This imbalance, like a car with a gas leak, results in a net positive energy balance and weight gain.
  • Circadian Misalignment and Metabolic Disruption: Circadian misalignment, a mismatch between our internal clock and the external world, also wreaks havoc on energy metabolism. It reduces 24-hour energy expenditure, particularly during sleep, and alters the rhythm of appetite hormones, promoting hunger and less healthy food choices. Even without changes in total calorie intake, consuming food at inappropriate circadian times, when the body's metabolic furnace is set to 'low,' can lead to weight gain.
  • Food Timing and Metabolic Health: The conclusion emphasizes that the timing of food intake, relative to both sleep and circadian rhythms, is a crucial factor in weight regulation. Eating late in the day, when the body's metabolic efficiency is naturally lower, is associated with reduced calorie burning and increased obesity risk. This highlights the importance of aligning meal times with the body's internal clock, even in the absence of other sleep disruptions.
