Overall Summary

Overview

The study investigates the structural differences in muscle fibers and myofibrils between individuals with long-term resistance training (LRT) experience and untrained individuals (UNT). By employing magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and muscle biopsies, the study assesses several muscle characteristics, such as anatomical cross-sectional area (ACSA), fiber area, fiber number, myofibril number, and myosin spacing. LRT individuals exhibited significantly greater muscle size and altered ultrastructure, suggesting that resistance training induces specific adaptations in muscle architecture, impacting both size and force production capabilities.

Key Findings

  • Enhanced Muscle Size: LRT participants demonstrated a 70% larger ACSA compared to UNT participants, measured using MRI, indicating significant muscle hypertrophy as a result of long-term training. This increased muscle size is crucial for improved force and power generation.
  • Increased Fiber Size and Number: Both type I and type II muscle fibers were larger in LRT individuals by 29%, and there were 34% more fibers, as determined by muscle biopsies, supporting the hypothesis of fiber hyperplasia as a response to resistance training.
  • Greater Myofibril Number: The study found a 49% increase in myofibril number per fiber and a total myofibril increase of 105% in LRT individuals, suggesting structural adaptations that enhance muscle contraction efficiency.
  • Tighter Myofilament Packing: LRT individuals showed reduced myosin spacing, indicating denser packing of myofilaments which can contribute to increased specific tension and muscle strength.
  • Correlations with ACSAmax: Positive correlations were observed between ACSAmax and average fiber area, fiber number, and myofibril count, whereas a negative correlation existed with myofibril area and myosin spacing, highlighting the interconnected nature of these structural adaptations.

Strengths

  • Comprehensive Methodology: The study employs a robust methodology, using MRI and muscle biopsies to gather detailed quantitative data on muscle structure, allowing for precise comparisons between LRT and UNT groups.
  • Clear Definition of Study Groups: Inclusion criteria for LRT participants are well-defined, ensuring a distinct comparison group which strengthens the validity of the results.
  • Detailed Statistical Analysis: The study rigorously uses appropriate statistical tests and effect size calculations, enhancing the reliability of the conclusions drawn from the data.
  • Integration of Multiple Measures: By examining both fiber and myofibril characteristics, the study provides a holistic view of muscle adaptations to resistance training, contributing to a comprehensive understanding of muscle hypertrophy.

Areas for Improvement

  • Clarify Training Protocols: Including specific details about the resistance training regimens (e.g., frequency, intensity) would provide better context for interpreting the findings and improve the study's applicability.
  • Acknowledge Study Limitations: The study would benefit from a more explicit acknowledgment of the limitations of its cross-sectional design, including its inability to establish causality, which would enhance the scientific rigor.
  • Provide Rationale for Sample Size: A power analysis or justification for the chosen sample size would ensure that the study is adequately powered to detect differences and reinforce the study's methodological integrity.

Conclusion

The study provides compelling evidence that long-term resistance training induces significant structural adaptations in skeletal muscles, including increased muscle size, fiber number, and myofibril density. These changes likely enhance muscle strength and power, supporting the practical benefits of resistance training for athletic performance and overall health. Future research should explore these adaptations longitudinally to verify causality and examine the role of specific training variables. Additionally, advanced imaging technologies could provide further insights into muscle plasticity and the mechanisms underlying these structural changes.

Section Analysis

Abstract

Overview

This abstract summarizes a study comparing muscle fiber and myofibril characteristics between long-term resistance-trained (LRT) and untrained (UNT) individuals. LRT individuals demonstrated significantly greater maximal anatomical cross-sectional area (ACSAmax), fiber area, fiber number, and myofibril number (total and per fiber). They also exhibited tighter myofilament packing (smaller myosin spacing) and a tendency toward smaller myofibril area. These findings suggest that long-term resistance training influences muscle ultrastructure, impacting both fiber size and number, as well as myofibril characteristics.

Key Aspects

  • Subject Groups: The study compares long-term resistance-trained (LRT) individuals with untrained (UNT) individuals.
  • Measurements: Measurements included ACSAmax (via MRI), muscle fiber area, myofibril area, and myosin spacing (via muscle biopsy). Fiber and myofibril numbers were estimated.
  • Key Findings: LRT individuals had significantly greater ACSAmax, fiber area, fiber number, and myofibril number. They also had smaller myosin spacing and a tendency toward smaller myofibril area.
  • Conclusion: The study concludes that long-term resistance training leads to changes in muscle ultrastructure, including increased fiber number and alterations in myofibril characteristics.

Strengths

  • Clear and concise

    The abstract effectively summarizes the study's purpose, methods, results, and conclusions in a concise manner.

    "Long-Term Resistance Trained Human Muscles Have More Fibers, More Myofibrils, and Tighter Myofilament Packing Than Untrained" (Page 1)
  • Quantifiable results

    "Compared with UNT, LRT individuals had greater ACSAmax (+70%, P < 0.001), fiber area (+29%, P = 0.028), fiber number (+34%, P = 0.013), and myofibril number per fiber (+49%, P = 0.034) and in total (+105%, P < 0.001)." (Page 1)

Suggestions for Improvement

  • Include a brief mention of the training protocols

    While the abstract mentions "long-term resistance training," it lacks specifics about the training protocols (e.g., frequency, intensity, type of exercises).

    "long-term resistance-trained (LRT)" (Page 1)

    Rationale: Providing a glimpse into the training characteristics would enhance the context and allow readers to better interpret the findings.

    Implementation: Add a brief phrase like "(e.g., training 3 times/week for 5+ years)" after mentioning LRT.

  • Clarify the limitations

    The abstract doesn't mention any limitations of the cross-sectional study design.

    "This cross-sectional study compared muscle fiber and myofibril characteristics in long-term resistance-trained (LRT) versus untrained (UNT) individuals." (Page 1)

    Rationale: Acknowledging limitations, such as the inability to establish causality with a cross-sectional design, strengthens the scientific rigor.

    Implementation: Add a sentence at the end acknowledging the limitations of the cross-sectional design and suggesting future longitudinal research to explore causal relationships.

Methods

Overview

This section details the methods employed in the study comparing muscle characteristics between long-term resistance-trained (LRT) and untrained (UNT) participants. It describes the participant selection criteria, including age, health status, and training experience (for LRT). The methods for data acquisition are outlined, encompassing magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) for muscle anatomical cross-sectional area (ACSA), muscle biopsies for fiber type and ultrastructure analysis, and questionnaires for physical activity and training routines. The section also details the procedures for muscle sample preparation, image analysis, and statistical analysis.

Key Aspects

  • Participants: 16 LRT (5.9 ± 3.5 years of RT experience) and 13 UNT male participants aged 18-40, healthy, with no history of steroid use or recent major injuries. LRT participants had extensive upper arm RT history (≥2 sessions/week, ≥10 months/year, ≥3 years) and met specific strength criteria (elbow flexion MVT >90 N·m and MVT/body mass >1.1 (N·m)·kg−1). UNT participants had no systematic physical training history.
  • MRI: T1-weighted axial MRI of the non-dominant arm was performed using a 3T scanner. Images were acquired from humeral head to below the elbow, with specific parameters detailed (TR, TE, FOV, matrix, pixel size, slice thickness). ACSA of the biceps brachii was segmented, and the maximum value (ACSAmax) was used for analysis.
  • Muscle Biopsy: Biopsies were taken from the distal biceps brachii of the non-dominant arm. Samples were prepared for immunohistochemistry (muscle fiber analysis) and transmission electron microscopy (ultrastructure analysis).
  • Muscle Fiber Analysis: Serial muscle sections were stained for myosin heavy chain I and analyzed using fluorescence microscopy. Fiber cross-sectional area and fiber type composition (type I and II) were determined.
  • Ultrastructure Analysis: Muscle samples were prepared for transmission electron microscopy. Myofibril cross-sectional area, myofibril number per fiber and per muscle, and myosin spacing were measured.
  • Statistical Analysis: Data were checked for outliers and normality. Mann-Whitney U tests or unpaired t-tests were used for group comparisons. Effect sizes were calculated using Cohen's d. Bivariate relationships were assessed using Pearson's correlations.

Strengths

  • Detailed Procedures

    The methods section provides a comprehensive and detailed description of the experimental procedures, including participant characteristics, MRI and biopsy protocols, and image analysis techniques. This level of detail enhances the reproducibility of the study.

    "T1-weighted axial magnetic resonance imaging of the nondominant arm was obtained with a 3T scanner (Discovery MR750w; GE Healthcare, Chicago, IL) and a receiver 16-channel flex coil." (Page 3)
  • Specific Criteria

    The study clearly defines the inclusion criteria for both LRT and UNT groups, ensuring a well-defined comparison. The specific strength criteria for LRT participants further strengthens the study's rigor.

    "LRT participants needed to have an extensive history of upper arm RT, specifically ≥2 sessions per week for ≥10 months·yr−1 and ≥3 yr, as well as elbow flexion maximum voluntary torque (MVT) of >90 N·m and MVT·kg−1 body mass of >1.1 (N·m)·kg−1 (9,10,30)." (Page 3)

Suggestions for Improvement

  • Blinding

    While the methods mention blinding during image analysis, it's unclear whether other aspects of the study were blinded, such as participant allocation or data collection. Specifying all instances of blinding would enhance the study's rigor.

    "Image analysis was undertaken using Fiji (v.2.0.0) software (33), and the investigator was blinded to the participant code of each sample." (Page 3)

    Rationale: Blinding helps minimize bias and strengthens the validity of the results.

    Implementation: Explicitly state whether blinding was implemented in other stages of the study, such as participant allocation or data collection. If not, acknowledge this as a limitation.

  • Rationale for Sample Size

    The methods section doesn't provide a rationale for the chosen sample size (16 LRT and 13 UNT). Including a power analysis or justification for the sample size would strengthen the study's methodological rigor.

    "Sixteen LRT (5.9 ± 3.5 yr of RT experience) and 13 UNT participants were recruited and completed the study." (Page 3)

    Rationale: A power analysis or justification helps ensure that the study is adequately powered to detect meaningful differences between groups.

    Implementation: Include a power analysis or a justification for the chosen sample size, explaining how it ensures sufficient statistical power.

Results

Overview

This section presents the findings of the study comparing muscle characteristics between long-term resistance-trained (LRT) and untrained (UNT) men. Key results include larger muscle ACSAmax, increased fiber area and number, greater myofibril number (total and per fiber) with a tendency towards smaller myofibril area, and tighter myosin spacing in LRT compared to UNT. Correlations between ACSAmax and various muscle structural parameters are also reported, generally supporting the hypothesized relationships between muscle size, fiber characteristics, and myofibril organization.

Key Aspects

  • Group Characteristics: LRT individuals were slightly older and had significantly greater body mass and IPAQ values (physical activity) compared to UNT. No significant differences were found in height or humerus length.
  • Muscle ACSAmax: Biceps brachii ACSAmax was 70% larger in LRT compared to UNT. Normalizing to humerus length did not alter this difference. ACSA at the biopsy site yielded identical findings.
  • Fiber Type Composition: No significant differences in muscle fiber type composition (proportion of type I and type II fibers) were found between groups, whether expressed by number or area.
  • Fiber Size and Number: Both type I and type II fiber areas were significantly larger in LRT compared to UNT. Consequently, average fiber area and total fiber number were also greater in LRT.
  • Myofibril Size and Number: Myofibril area tended to be smaller in LRT, while myofibril number per fiber and total myofibril number within the muscle were significantly greater in LRT.
  • Myosin Spacing: Myosin spacing was significantly smaller in LRT, indicating tighter myofilament packing.
  • Correlations: Significant positive correlations were found between ACSAmax and average fiber area, fiber number, myofibril number per fiber, and total myofibril number. A significant negative correlation was found between ACSAmax and myofibril area and myosin spacing.

Strengths

  • Clear Presentation of Results

    The results are presented clearly and systematically, with appropriate statistical analyses and effect size calculations. The use of tables and figures enhances the clarity and accessibility of the data.

    "Data are group mean ± SD. n = 16 for LRT and n = 13 for UNT. *Significant difference between groups." (Page 4)
  • Comprehensive Analysis

    The study analyzes multiple aspects of muscle structure, including fiber type composition, size, and number, as well as myofibril size, number, and myosin spacing. This comprehensive approach provides a detailed understanding of the effects of LRT on muscle ultrastructure.

    "These findings mostly supported our hypotheses and suggest that the larger muscles of LRT individuals were characterized as having muscle fibers of greater size and number in cross section, which likely contain smaller but more myofibrils as well as more tightly packed myofilaments." (Page 6)

Suggestions for Improvement

  • Individual Data Presentation

    While the figures include individual data points, the text primarily reports mean ± SD. Presenting individual data in a tabular format or as supplementary material would provide a more complete picture of the data distribution and variability.

    "All data are presented as mean ± SD in the text, and individual data together with box and whisker plots are shown within the figures (detailed in each figure)." (Page 4)

    Rationale: This would allow for a more detailed examination of the data and could reveal potential outliers or interesting individual variations.

    Implementation: Include a table with individual data points for each variable or provide this data as supplementary material.

  • Discussion of Non-Significant Trends

    The results mention a "tendency toward smaller myofibril area" in LRT. While not statistically significant, this trend could be biologically relevant. A brief discussion of this trend and its potential implications would strengthen the analysis.

    "There was a tendency toward smaller myofibril area (−16%, 0.628 ± 0.204 vs 0.748 ± 0.192 μm2, P = 0.074, d = 0.61, “medium”; Fig. 5A) for LRT versus UNT." (Page 5)

    Rationale: This would provide a more nuanced interpretation of the findings and could generate hypotheses for future research.

    Implementation: Add a sentence or two in the Discussion section discussing the non-significant trend in myofibril area and its potential biological relevance.

Discussion

Overview

This section discusses the findings of the study, focusing on the adaptations observed in long-term resistance-trained (LRT) individuals compared to untrained (UNT) individuals. The discussion highlights the greater muscle size, fiber area and number, myofibril number, and myofilament packing density in LRT individuals. It also addresses potential explanations for these adaptations, including muscle fiber hyperplasia and myofibril proliferation/splitting. The limitations of the cross-sectional study design are acknowledged, particularly the inability to establish causality. Suggestions for future research, such as longitudinal studies and advanced imaging techniques, are proposed.

Key Aspects

  • Muscle Size and Fiber Characteristics: LRT individuals exhibited substantially larger biceps brachii muscles, explained by both increased fiber area and a greater number of fibers. The increase in fiber number suggests potential muscle fiber hyperplasia, although longitudinal studies are needed to confirm this.
  • Myofibrils and Myofilaments: LRT individuals had a greater number of myofibrils, likely contributing to their higher force and power production. Smaller myofibril area and tighter myosin spacing (greater packing density) were also observed, potentially enhancing specific tension and strength.
  • Study Limitations: The cross-sectional design limits the ability to establish causality between LRT and the observed adaptations. The estimation of fiber and myofibril numbers, while based on previous studies, involves assumptions about muscle and fiber composition. The correlation analysis may be confounded by circularity.
  • Future Research Directions: Longitudinal studies with multiple measurements are needed to determine the causal relationship between LRT and muscle adaptations. Advanced imaging techniques, such as diffusion tensor magnetic resonance imaging, could provide non-invasive estimations of fiber characteristics and further insights into muscle plasticity.
