Overall Summary

Overview

This study investigated the associations of physical activity (PA) intensity, volume, duration, and fragmentation with all-cause and cardiovascular disease (CVD) mortality risk in US adults using data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES). The study also aimed to establish reference values for PA volume and intensity within the US adult population by creating centile curves.

Key Findings

  • PA intensity, as measured by the intensity gradient (IG), was a stronger predictor of both all-cause and CVD mortality than PA volume (average acceleration, AvAcc).
  • Accumulating intense PA in continuous bouts, even as short as 5 minutes, was associated with lower all-cause mortality compared to accumulating the same amount of PA in shorter, more sporadic bursts.
  • Age- and sex-specific centile curves for AvAcc and IG showed a decline in both intensity and volume with increasing age.
  • The proportion of individuals meeting PA levels associated with reduced mortality risk decreased considerably with age.
  • The relationship between PA intensity/volume and mortality risk plateaued at a certain level, indicating potential optimal levels for health benefits.

Strengths

  • The study used a nationally representative sample from NHANES, enhancing the generalizability of the findings to the US adult population.
  • The study employed a comprehensive set of PA metrics derived from accelerometer data, including novel measures like MXRATIO, to capture different dimensions of PA.
  • The use of validated accelerometer metrics and robust statistical methods, including weighted Cox proportional hazards regression and natural splines, strengthens the validity and reliability of the findings.
  • The generation of age- and sex-specific centile curves provides valuable reference values for assessing PA levels and informing personalized recommendations.

Areas for Improvement

  • The cross-sectional design limits the ability to infer causal relationships between PA and mortality; future research using longitudinal data is needed to confirm the findings.
  • The potential impact of unmeasured covariates, such as genetic predisposition or detailed dietary habits, on the observed associations could be further explored.
  • The generalizability of the findings to other populations with different cultural backgrounds or socioeconomic status could be investigated in future studies.

Conclusion

This study highlights the importance of PA intensity for reducing mortality risk in US adults. Higher PA intensity, rather than just volume, is associated with lower all-cause and CVD mortality. Accumulating intense PA in continuous bouts may be more beneficial than fragmented PA. The generated centile curves offer valuable benchmarks for assessing PA levels and informing personalized recommendations to promote health and longevity.

Methods and results

Overview

This section details the methodology employed in the study, including the data source, population, outcome measures, accelerometer data processing, physical activity metrics, and statistical analysis techniques. It then presents the key findings regarding the association of physical activity intensity, volume, duration, and fragmentation with mortality risk, as well as the development of reference values and centile curves for physical activity volume and intensity.

Key Aspects

  • Data Source and Population: The study utilized data from the 2011-2014 National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) linked to the National Death Index, focusing on adults aged 20 years and older.
  • Outcome Measures: The primary outcome was all-cause mortality, and the secondary outcome was cardiovascular disease (CVD) mortality.
  • Accelerometry and Data Processing: Physical activity was measured using ActiGraph GT3X+ accelerometers worn on the non-dominant wrist for 7 days. Data were processed using the GGIR R-package, and participants with insufficient valid wear time were excluded.

  • Statistical Analysis: Weighted Cox proportional hazards regression analyses were conducted to examine the associations between physical activity metrics and mortality risk. Centile curves for AvAcc and IG were generated using the LMS method.

Results

Overview

This section presents the findings of the study, focusing on the associations between physical activity (PA) metrics and mortality risk, as well as the development of reference values for PA volume and intensity in the US adult population. The results indicate that PA intensity, particularly the intensity gradient (IG), is a stronger predictor of both all-cause and cardiovascular disease (CVD) mortality than PA volume (average acceleration, AvAcc). The study also found that accumulating intense PA in continuous bouts is associated with lower mortality risk compared to fragmented PA. Age- and sex-specific centile curves for AvAcc and IG are presented, highlighting the decline in PA intensity and volume with age.

Key Aspects

  • All-Cause Mortality: Both AvAcc and IG were inversely associated with all-cause mortality, with IG showing a stronger predictive power. The relationships plateaued at ~35-45 mg for AvAcc and -2.7 to -2.5 for IG.
  • CVD Mortality: IG was strongly inversely associated with CVD mortality, while AvAcc showed a weaker association. The relationship between IG and CVD mortality also plateaued at -2.7 to -2.5.
  • PA Fragmentation: Accumulating intense PA in continuous bouts was associated with lower all-cause mortality risk compared to fragmented PA, even when controlling for intensity and volume.
  • Reference Values: Age- and sex-specific centile curves for AvAcc and IG were generated, showing a decline in both intensity and volume with age. The curves also highlight the proportion of individuals meeting PA levels associated with reduced mortality risk, which decreases considerably with age.

Strengths

  • Thorough Statistical Analysis

    The study employs robust statistical methods, including weighted Cox proportional hazards regression and natural splines, to analyze the associations between PA metrics and mortality. The use of these methods strengthens the validity and reliability of the findings.

    "We ran weighted Cox proportional hazards regression analyses to answer study aim 1. Models were adjusted for age (continuous), sex (woman/man), household income (<$25k, $25k-$75k, >$75k, other), BMI (continuous), ethnicity (Mexican American, other Hispanic, non-Hispanic White, non-Hispanic Black, and other race—including multi-racial), education status (below high school, high school, and above high school), diabetes (yes/no, borderline = yes), congestive heart failure (yes/no), coronary heart disease (yes/no), angina pectoris (yes/no), heart attack (yes/no), stroke (yes/no), and cancer (yes/no)." (Page 4)
  • Detailed Reporting of Results

    The section provides a comprehensive and detailed account of the study's findings, including model fit statistics, hazard ratios, confidence intervals, and p-values. This level of detail enhances the transparency and allows readers to critically evaluate the evidence.

    "An inverse curvilinear relationship with all-cause mortality was observed for both IG, plateauing at −2.7 to −2.5, and AvAcc, stabilizing at ∼35–45 mg, beyond which no further risk alterations were noted (Figure 1A and B). Data scarcity at distribution tails limits precise dose–response quantification. For IG, hazard ratios decreased by −37.1% (95% CI, −30.0 to −43.4%) from 25th to 50th percentile and −18.8% (95%, −43.0 to +15.5%) from 50th to 75th percentile." (Page 4)

Discussion

Overview

This section discusses the study's key findings regarding the association of physical activity (PA) intensity and volume with mortality risk in US adults. It highlights that PA intensity, as reflected by the intensity gradient (IG), is a stronger predictor of both all-cause and cardiovascular disease (CVD) mortality than PA volume (average acceleration, AvAcc). The discussion also emphasizes the importance of accumulating intense PA in continuous bouts rather than sporadically for reducing mortality risk. The section further discusses the generated centile curves for AvAcc and IG, which offer benchmarks for assessing PA levels in the US adult population and provide a basis for personalized recommendations. The limitations of the study are also acknowledged, including the cross-sectional design and potential biases related to accelerometer calibration errors.

Key Aspects

  • Intensity vs. Volume: The study found that PA intensity is a stronger predictor of mortality risk than PA volume, particularly for CVD mortality. This suggests that the distribution of PA intensity throughout the day is crucial for longevity.
  • PA Fragmentation: Accumulating intense PA in continuous bouts, even as short as 5 minutes, was associated with greater reductions in all-cause mortality compared to the same amount of PA accumulated sporadically. This challenges the notion that "every minute counts" equally, regardless of PA pattern.
  • Reference Values: The generated age- and sex-specific centile curves for AvAcc and IG provide valuable benchmarks for assessing PA levels in US adults. These curves also highlight the decline in PA intensity and volume with age and the decreasing proportion of individuals meeting PA levels associated with reduced mortality risk.
  • Comparison with Other Populations: The study compared the PA patterns of US adults with those of healthy Swiss adults and UK Biobank participants, revealing differences in intensity distribution and volume. These comparisons suggest that US adults may have a less favorable PA profile, potentially due to factors such as mobility limitations and accelerometer wear site.
  • Limitations: The discussion acknowledges the limitations of the study, including the cross-sectional design, potential biases related to accelerometer calibration errors, and the possibility of residual bias due to unmeasured covariates.
