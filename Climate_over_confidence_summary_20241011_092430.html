Overall Summary

Overview

This study examines the risks and implications of "overshoot pathways," where global temperatures temporarily exceed the Paris Agreement's warming limits before being brought back down. The authors argue that overshoot scenarios present significantly different and often irreversible climate risks compared to pathways that avoid exceeding the limits. Using various climate models and scenarios, they demonstrate that achieving declining temperatures after an overshoot is crucial for limiting long-term risks but also highlight the substantial uncertainties involved in temperature reversal, particularly concerning the effectiveness and feasibility of large-scale carbon dioxide removal (CDR). The study emphasizes the need for rapid near-term emission reductions to minimize reliance on CDR and mitigate climate risks effectively.

Key Findings

  • Climate Response Uncertainty and CDR Needs: The amount of CDR required to reverse warming after an overshoot is highly uncertain and depends on the climate's sensitivity to greenhouse gas emissions. Using the FaIR model, the study demonstrates that some scenarios may require hundreds of gigatonnes of CO2 removal, exceeding current technological capabilities and raising feasibility concerns. This implies that we need to plan for a range of possible warming outcomes, not just the most likely one, to adequately prepare for and mitigate the risks of overshoot.
  • Regional Climate Change Irreversibility: Even if global temperatures decline after an overshoot, regional climates may not simply revert to their previous states. Model simulations reveal persistent regional differences in temperature and precipitation patterns between overshoot and stabilization scenarios, particularly in the North Atlantic and adjacent European regions, due to the time-lagged response of the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC). This implies that regional impacts can be more severe and long-lasting than predicted by global averages, requiring region-specific adaptation strategies.
  • Irreversible Impacts Across Earth Systems: Certain climate impacts, such as sea-level rise, changes in the deep ocean, and shifts in ecosystems, are not readily reversible, even with declining global temperatures. Sea levels, in particular, are projected to continue rising for centuries to millennia due to thermal expansion and ice sheet melt, posing significant long-term threats to coastal communities. This implies that some consequences of overshoot are locked in for generations, highlighting the need to avoid exceeding temperature limits in the first place.
  • Socioeconomic Impacts and Adaptive Capacity: The severity of overshoot impacts depends heavily on the adaptive capacity of human systems. Low adaptive capacity, particularly in the near term and in low-income countries, can amplify climate risks, leading to lasting negative consequences for human well-being, economic performance, and intergenerational equity. This implies that prioritizing adaptation measures and enhancing resilience, especially for vulnerable populations, is essential for mitigating the societal and economic burdens of overshoot.
  • Adaptation Planning Challenges: The long timescales involved in potential impact reversals (decades to centuries) often exceed the typical planning horizons of adaptation strategies and infrastructure lifecycles. This makes it challenging to incorporate the potential benefits of long-term temperature decline into near-term adaptation decisions. This implies that current adaptation planning should primarily focus on managing the risks associated with peak warming, rather than relying on uncertain future reversals.

Strengths

  • Comprehensive Risk Assessment: The study considers a wide range of climate risks associated with overshoot, including biophysical, socioeconomic, and regional impacts. This holistic approach provides a more complete picture of the potential consequences of exceeding temperature limits.
  • Use of Multiple Models and Scenarios: The study employs various climate models and emission scenarios, including the FaIR model, ESMs like GFDL-ESM2M and NorESM2-LM, and CMIP6 projections. This multi-model approach enhances the robustness of the findings and accounts for uncertainties in climate projections.
  • Emphasis on Uncertainty: The study explicitly addresses the uncertainties inherent in climate projections and CDR deployment. This transparent handling of uncertainty strengthens the credibility of the analysis and highlights the need for caution in interpreting the results.
  • Clear Ethical Focus: The study clearly articulates the ethical implications of overshoot, particularly concerning intergenerational equity and the disproportionate burden on vulnerable populations. This ethical focus adds an important dimension to the discussion and underscores the social justice aspects of climate change.

Areas for Improvement

  • Quantifying Irreversible Impacts: While the study identifies numerous irreversible impacts, providing more quantitative estimates of their magnitude and duration would strengthen the analysis. Future research could focus on developing metrics and methods for quantifying irreversible changes across different Earth systems.
  • Exploring Alternative Pathways: The study primarily focuses on a limited set of emission scenarios. Expanding the analysis to include a wider range of pathways, including those with more ambitious near-term emission reductions, would provide a more comprehensive understanding of overshoot risks and adaptation needs.

Conclusion

This study provides compelling evidence that overshoot pathways present distinct and often irreversible climate risks compared to scenarios that avoid exceeding temperature limits. The uncertainty surrounding temperature reversal, the potentially massive scale of CDR required, and the lasting regional and socioeconomic impacts underscore the need for a precautionary approach to climate policy. Future research should focus on quantifying irreversible impacts, exploring alternative emission pathways, and developing robust adaptation strategies that account for the long-term consequences of overshoot. Critically, the study highlights the ethical imperative to minimize climate risks and prioritize rapid, deep emission reductions to avoid exceeding temperature limits in the first place, rather than relying on uncertain and potentially infeasible technological solutions to reverse warming after an overshoot.

Conceptual categories of peak and decline emission pathways

Overview

This section introduces a new framework for categorizing emission pathways based on their peak temperature and subsequent decline characteristics. It argues that the existing categorization, primarily based on peak temperature outcomes, is insufficient for understanding the nuances of pathways aligned with the Paris Agreement. The proposed framework distinguishes between three pathway categories: Peak and Decline (PD), Overshoot (PD-OS), and Enhanced Protection (PD-EP) pathways. Each category is defined by its temperature trajectory and corresponding emission reduction strategies, emphasizing the importance of considering both peak warming and the rate and duration of temperature decline.

Key Aspects

  • Peak and Decline (PD) Pathways: These pathways aim for a temperature peak followed by a sustained decline over several decades. They involve emission reductions in all greenhouse gases and require net-negative CO2 emissions after reaching net-zero.
  • Overshoot (PD-OS) Pathways: A subset of PD pathways, these allow for exceeding a target warming level (e.g., 1.5°C) in the near term with the expectation of reversing warming later. The rate of emission reduction and the amount of net-negative emissions depend on the characteristics of the overshoot.
  • Enhanced Protection (PD-EP) Pathways: These pathways prioritize minimizing peak warming and promoting a gradual temperature reversal to reduce climate risks. They involve stringent and rapid emission reductions, aiming for net-zero CO2 emissions as soon as possible and sustainable net-negative CO2 emissions thereafter.

Strengths

  • Clear Framework

    The section presents a clear and well-defined framework for categorizing emission pathways based on peak and decline characteristics. This provides a more nuanced understanding of different pathway types and their implications.

    "Because a peak and gradual reversal of global warming turns out to be a fundamental feature of Paris-compatible pathways16, we propose to henceforth categorize pathways in terms of their peak and decline characteristics (Table 1)." (Page 2)
  • Relevance to Paris Agreement

    The section explicitly links the proposed framework to the Paris Agreement, highlighting its importance for understanding and achieving the agreement's temperature goals.

    "PD pathways establish a target warming level to be achieved at some point in the far future but allow it to be exceeded with high likelihood over the near term in the conviction that warming can be reversed again at a later stage." (Page 2)

Uncertain climate response and reversal

Overview

This section explores the uncertainties associated with predicting how the climate will respond to emission changes, particularly in overshoot scenarios. It emphasizes that simply focusing on the median, or most likely, warming outcome can lead to underestimating the potential risks and the amount of carbon dioxide removal (CDR) needed to bring temperatures back down. The section uses a model to illustrate how different levels of climate sensitivity could result in significantly different warming outcomes, even with the same emission pathway. It also highlights the uncertainty in how much CDR would be required to return to 1.5°C warming by 2100, showing that hundreds of gigatonnes of CO2 removal might be necessary in some scenarios. Essentially, the section argues that we need to consider the full range of possible warming outcomes, not just the most likely one, to adequately plan for and mitigate the risks of overshoot.

Key Aspects

  • Uncertainty in Climate Response: The section highlights the importance of considering uncertainties in climate response, including both the warming phase and the long-term state of the climate system after emissions peak. This uncertainty arises from factors like the strength of feedback loops and the effectiveness of CDR.
  • Net-Negative CO2 Emissions (NNCE) Requirements: The section explores the NNCE requirements for an illustrative pathway that achieves net-zero CO2 emissions around mid-century and limits median peak warming close to 1.5°C. It finds that the required NNCE can vary significantly depending on the climate response, with some scenarios requiring large-scale CDR deployment.
  • Climate Response Uncertainty Distribution: The analysis reveals a heavy-tailed distribution for climate response uncertainty, meaning that high warming outcomes are more likely than previously thought. This has significant implications for NNCE requirements and the feasibility of achieving temperature reversal.
  • Limitations of Median Warming Focus: The section criticizes the common practice of focusing on median warming outcomes, arguing that this can lead to underestimation of potential risks and CDR needs. It emphasizes the importance of considering the full range of plausible warming outcomes.
