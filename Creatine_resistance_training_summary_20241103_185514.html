Overall Summary

Overview

Creatine supplementation significantly improves both upper and lower body strength (WMD = 4.43 kg and 11.35 kg, respectively). The meta-analysis utilizes WMD, a methodological strength. Key limitations include potential publication bias (evidenced by funnel plot asymmetry) and study heterogeneity. The inclusion of forest plots and the exploration of sex differences enhance the analysis.

Section: Discussion

Conclusion

The meta-analysis demonstrates a statistically significant positive effect of creatine supplementation on both upper and lower body strength, with a weighted mean difference (WMD) of 4.43 kg for upper body and 11.35 kg for lower body. The use of WMD is a methodological strength, allowing for direct interpretation of the effect size in kg. However, limitations such as potential publication bias (indicated by the funnel plot asymmetry) and the heterogeneity of included studies warrant cautious interpretation. The exploration of sex differences and the inclusion of forest plots enhance the analysis, contributing valuable insights into the effects of creatine supplementation.

Section Analysis

Abstract

Key Aspects

  • Study Objective and Hypothesis: This meta-analysis investigated the impact of creatine supplementation combined with resistance training on muscle strength in adults under 50. It aimed to address limitations of previous meta-analyses by focusing on a specific age group and using weighted mean differences (WMD) in kilograms to quantify strength gains. This approach allows for a more practical interpretation of the results compared to using standardized mean differences (SMD). The study hypothesized that creatine supplementation with resistance training would lead to greater muscle strength gains compared to resistance training with a placebo.
  • Methodology: The researchers searched four databases: MEDLINE, Scopus, Embase, and SPORTDiscus, with a search end date of May 22, 2024. Twenty-three randomized controlled trials (RCTs) met the inclusion criteria. These criteria included studies comparing creatine supplementation combined with resistance training to a placebo combined with resistance training in healthy adults under 50. The primary outcome was the change in maximal muscle strength, measured pre- and post-intervention, primarily using one-repetition maximum (1RM) tests. This focus on 1RM provides a direct measure of maximal strength capacity.
  • Main Findings: Creatine supplementation combined with resistance training significantly increased both upper and lower body strength compared to placebo. The weighted mean difference (WMD) for upper body strength was 4.43 kg, and for lower body strength was 11.35 kg, both with p < 0.001. Subgroup analyses revealed a trend for greater upper body strength improvements in males compared to females using creatine (p=0.067). Males on creatine significantly increased both upper and lower body strength, while females showed no significant gains. A trend towards greater lower body strength gains was observed with higher doses of creatine (p=0.068). No other variables significantly influenced the effect of creatine.
  • Conclusions: The study concludes that creatine supplementation combined with resistance training enhances both upper and lower body muscle strength in adults under 50, with greater benefits observed in males. These findings suggest that creatine is a beneficial ergogenic aid for increasing muscle strength when used in conjunction with resistance training. The study highlights the need for further research to explore sex-specific responses to creatine and to optimize resistance training protocols for maximizing strength gains with creatine supplementation.

2. Methods

Key Aspects

  • Search Strategy: The authors conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines and registered the protocol on PROSPERO. They searched four databases from inception until May 22, 2024, using keywords related to creatine and resistance training. This systematic approach ensures a comprehensive and unbiased search for relevant studies.
  • Eligibility Criteria: Studies were included if they were randomized controlled trials (RCTs) involving healthy individuals under 50, comparing creatine supplementation with resistance training to placebo with resistance training. The primary outcome was muscle strength gains measured before and after the intervention. These criteria ensure the inclusion of high-quality studies directly addressing the research question.
  • Study Selection Process: Two independent reviewers screened titles and abstracts, resolving discrepancies through discussion. Inter-rater reliability was high (kappa = 0.83). This process minimizes bias and ensures consistency in study selection.
  • Quality Assessment: Methodological quality was assessed using the PEDro scale by two independent reviewers, with a high inter-rater reliability (kappa = 0.91). The average PEDro score was 7.00 Â± 0.95, indicating good quality. This assessment provides an indication of the methodological rigor of the included studies.
  • Data Extraction: Data extracted included study characteristics, participant demographics, creatine supplementation details (dose, duration), resistance training characteristics (frequency, duration), and pre- and post-intervention muscle strength measurements (primarily 1RM). Authors were contacted for missing data. This comprehensive data extraction allows for a thorough analysis of the effects of creatine.
  • Data Analysis: Weighted mean differences (WMD) in kilograms were used to quantify the effect of creatine on muscle strength. Heterogeneity was assessed using the I2 statistic. Subgroup analyses were conducted based on creatine characteristics, training characteristics, and participant characteristics. Publication bias was assessed using funnel plots and Egger's regression test. These analyses provide a robust and nuanced understanding of the effects of creatine.

3. Results

Key Aspects

  • Study Selection: From an initial pool of 1586 records, 23 randomized controlled trials (RCTs) met the inclusion criteria after removing duplicates and screening titles, abstracts, and full texts. These 23 studies formed the basis of the meta-analysis. This rigorous selection process ensures that the included studies are relevant to the research question and of sufficient quality.
  • Methodological Quality: The included studies were assessed for methodological quality using the PEDro scale. The average PEDro score was 7.00, indicating "good" methodological quality. Two studies were rated as "excellent," and the remaining 21 were rated as "good." This assessment provides an indication of the internal validity and reliability of the included studies.
  • Participant Characteristics: The 23 included studies involved a total of 487 participants. Most studies involved male participants (n=447), with only two studies exclusively involving females (n=40) and one study including both males and females (n=22). The participant characteristics are important for understanding the generalizability of the findings and potential sex-specific effects.
  • Upper-Body Strength: Main Finding: Creatine supplementation combined with resistance training led to significantly greater increases in upper-body strength compared to placebo (WMD = 4.43 kg, p < 0.001). The I-squared value was 0%, indicating no significant heterogeneity between studies. This finding supports the hypothesis that creatine supplementation enhances strength gains in the upper body.
  • Lower-Body Strength: Main Finding: Creatine supplementation combined with resistance training also resulted in significantly greater increases in lower-body strength compared to placebo (WMD = 11.35 kg, p < 0.001). Similar to upper-body strength, there was no significant heterogeneity between studies (I-squared = 0%). This finding further strengthens the evidence for creatine's ergogenic effect on muscle strength.
  • Subgroup Analyses: Subgroup analyses were performed for both upper- and lower-body strength to explore the influence of various factors, including creatine loading protocol, creatine dose, training duration, training frequency, training status, and sex. While males experienced significant strength gains with creatine, females did not show significant improvements. There was also a trend towards greater lower-body strength gains with higher doses of creatine. These subgroup analyses provide insights into potential moderators of creatine's effects.
  • Publication Bias: Publication bias was assessed using funnel plots and Egger's regression test. A potential publication bias was detected for upper-body strength, but the adjusted effect size remained significant after applying the trim-and-fill method. No publication bias was detected for lower-body strength. Assessing publication bias is crucial for evaluating the robustness and reliability of the meta-analysis results.

Description
  • Purpose of funnel plot: Figure 6 presents a funnel plot, which is a scatterplot used in meta-analyses to visually assess publication bias. Publication bias arises when studies with statistically significant or positive results are more likely to be published than those with non-significant or negative results.
  • Elements of a funnel plot: In a funnel plot, each dot represents a study. The x-axis represents the study's effect size (strength of the effect of creatine in this case), and the y-axis represents a measure of the study's precision, usually the standard error. Studies with high precision will cluster near the top and the spread should widen as precision decreases. In the absence of publication bias, the points form a roughly symmetrical inverted funnel shape. Asymmetry, as seen in Figure 6, can suggest publication bias.
  • Trim and fill method: The trim and fill method is a technique used to address publication bias. It imputes "missing" studies (represented by black circles) that would make the funnel plot symmetrical if publication bias were the sole source of asymmetry. The white diamond at the bottom shows the original overall effect, and the black diamond shows the adjusted effect after accounting for the imputed studies.
Scientific Validity

  • Limitations of trim and fill: The trim and fill method is a widely used technique for adjusting for publication bias, but it is important to note that it relies on assumptions that may not always hold true. The results of the trim and fill analysis should be interpreted cautiously.
  • Reporting adjusted effect size: Providing the adjusted effect size (WMD after imputation) and its confidence interval would be valuable. Quantifying the impact of the imputed studies helps readers understand the extent to which publication bias may have influenced the overall effect estimate.
