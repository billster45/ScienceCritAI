Overall Summary

Overview

This systematic review investigates the parallel electrophysiological abnormalities detected by electroencephalography (EEG) in individuals with Long COVID and Alzheimer's disease and related dementias (ADRD). The review found that COVID-19 patients, even months after the initial infection, frequently display abnormal EEG activity, including a slowing of brain waves and epileptiform activity, similar to patterns observed in early ADRD. These shared EEG abnormalities suggest potential overlapping pathologies, such as neuroinflammation, astrocyte reactivity, hypoxia, and neurovascular injury. The review proposes that routine EEG could be a valuable tool for monitoring COVID-19 patients, predicting long-term outcomes, and assessing treatment effectiveness, offering insights into the shared mechanisms and potential therapeutic targets for both Long COVID and ADRD. The analysis included keyword searches across multiple databases, expert panel review, and consideration of co-authors' research and expert communications.

Key Findings

  • Generalized slowing of brain activity, particularly in the frontal regions, is a common EEG finding in both acute COVID-19 and Long COVID. This slowing is characterized by increased delta and theta power and decreased alpha power, mirroring EEG changes seen in early ADRD. This suggests a potential disruption of normal brain function and could contribute to cognitive symptoms like "brain fog." The slowing was measured by analyzing EEG recordings and quantifying the power in different frequency bands. The implications of this finding are that EEG could be used as a potential biomarker for identifying and monitoring neurological complications in COVID-19 patients.
  • More severe EEG alterations, such as epileptiform activity and alpha coma, are observed in COVID-19 patients with pre-existing conditions like epilepsy or cognitive impairment. This suggests a heightened vulnerability to neurological complications in these individuals. These severe EEG patterns were identified through visual inspection and analysis of EEG recordings. The implication is that patients with pre-existing neurological conditions should be closely monitored for neurological complications following COVID-19 infection.
  • Long COVID patients exhibit persistent EEG abnormalities, including reduced posterior alpha dominance and increased delta/theta activity, often with hemispheric asymmetries. These persistent changes suggest long-lasting effects of COVID-19 on brain function and could be related to ongoing cognitive difficulties. These EEG abnormalities were observed in studies that followed COVID-19 patients for several months after the initial infection. This highlights the need for long-term neurological follow-up and potential interventions for Long COVID patients.
  • The EEG abnormalities observed in Long COVID resemble those seen in mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and ADRD, suggesting shared neurophysiological mechanisms. This parallel could indicate a potential increased risk of developing ADRD in individuals with Long COVID. This similarity was established by comparing EEG patterns in Long COVID patients with those in individuals diagnosed with MCI and ADRD. This finding warrants further investigation into the long-term cognitive consequences of COVID-19 and potential preventative strategies.

Strengths

  • The review employed a comprehensive search strategy, utilizing multiple databases and specific keywords, to identify relevant literature on COVID-19 and EEG. This thorough approach ensures a wide coverage of the existing research and minimizes the risk of missing important studies. The use of multiple databases strengthens the reliability of the findings.
  • The involvement of an expert panel, specializing in both ADRD and COVID-19, ensured the quality and rigor of the review process. The expertise of the panel members enhances the credibility of the review and its conclusions. The panel's diverse expertise in neurophysiology, astrocyte reactivity, and neuroinflammation broadened the scope of the review.
  • The review clearly describes the neurological and cognitive symptoms associated with Long COVID, providing a comprehensive overview of the clinical manifestations of the disease. This detailed description helps readers understand the diverse ways in which Long COVID can affect patients. The use of statistics from multiple studies strengthens the evidence base for the prevalence of these symptoms.

Areas for Improvement

  • While the review suggests a potential link between COVID-19 and amyloid/tau pathologies, the evidence presented is largely based on parallels with ADRD research. Future research should directly investigate the presence and role of amyloid/tau pathology in Long COVID patients to strengthen this link. This could involve post-mortem brain studies or the use of biomarkers for amyloid/tau in living patients. This would provide more conclusive evidence for this potential connection.
  • The review mentions the potential of EEG as a diagnostic and prognostic tool in Long COVID, but it would be beneficial to discuss its limitations more explicitly. EEG has limited spatial resolution compared to other neuroimaging techniques like MRI, and EEG findings can be non-specific, meaning similar patterns can be seen in different conditions. Addressing these limitations would provide a more balanced perspective and guide future research to refine the use of EEG in this context.

Conclusion

This review highlights the potential of EEG as a valuable tool for understanding and managing the neurological complications of Long COVID. The findings suggest that Long COVID and ADRD share similar neurophysiological abnormalities, detectable through routine EEG, potentially stemming from overlapping pathophysiological mechanisms such as neuroinflammation, astrocyte reactivity, and synaptic dysfunction. Future research should focus on validating EEG as a biomarker for Long COVID severity and cognitive decline risk, investigating the long-term neurological consequences of COVID-19, and exploring potential therapeutic interventions targeting shared mechanisms with ADRD. Further research is crucial to determine the specificity of EEG abnormalities in Long COVID compared to other conditions, to establish standardized EEG protocols for Long COVID assessment, and to explore the impact of diverse demographic factors on EEG findings. This will pave the way for personalized medicine approaches and targeted interventions for individuals with Long COVID.

Abstract

Overview

This abstract summarizes a systematic review conducted by an expert panel on the parallel electrophysiological abnormalities observed in individuals with Long COVID and those with Alzheimer's disease and related dementias (ADRD). The review found that COVID-19 patients, even months after acute infection, often exhibit abnormal EEG activity, including slowing (reduced alpha, increased slow waves) and epileptiform activity, mirroring patterns seen in early ADRD. The panel proposes that these similar EEG abnormalities stem from parallel pathologies like neuroinflammation, astrocyte reactivity, hypoxia, and neurovascular injury. They suggest that routine EEG could be valuable for monitoring COVID-19 patients, predicting long-term outcomes, and assessing treatment efficacy.

Key Aspects

  • Abnormal EEG in Long COVID: Many COVID-19 patients exhibit abnormal EEG activity, including slowing and epileptiform activity, which reflects cognitive impairments like "brain fog."
  • EEG Similarities with ADRD: These EEG abnormalities in Long COVID resemble those seen in the early stages of ADRD.
  • Parallel Pathologies: The review suggests that overlapping pathologies, such as neuroinflammation and astrocyte reactivity, drive the similar EEG abnormalities in both conditions.
  • Potential of EEG as a Tool: The abstract highlights the potential of routine EEG exams for detecting these neurophysiological abnormalities in COVID-19 patients, which could be used for predicting long-term outcomes and monitoring treatment effectiveness.

Strengths

    The abstract effectively summarizes the key findings and the purpose of the review in a clear and concise manner.

Methodology of the Review

Overview

This section outlines the methodology employed for the systematic review. The review focused on EEG signatures of Long COVID, comparing them with existing literature on Alzheimer's disease and related dementias (ADRD). EEG Slowing in COVID-19

Overview

This section explores the potential reasons behind the EEG slowing observed in COVID-19 patients, drawing parallels with similar patterns seen in Alzheimer's disease and related dementias (ADRD). It discusses how amyloid and tau pathologies, often associated with ADRD, might be linked to COVID-19 and contribute to the observed EEG slowing. Additionally, the section investigates the role of hypoxia, heart rate variability (HRV), and their connection to abnormal EEG activity in Long COVID. It suggests that hypoxia, resulting from various factors including respiratory issues and heart-brain connection abnormalities, can lead to EEG slowing and cognitive symptoms like brain fog. The section also touches upon the EEG pattern of burst suppression, seen in some slow-recovering COVID-19 patients, and its association with reduced brain metabolism.

Key Aspects

  • Amyloid and Tau Pathologies: The section explores the potential link between COVID-19 and amyloid and tau pathologies, which are characteristic of ADRD and could contribute to EEG slowing.
  • Hypoxia and its Mechanisms: It discusses how hypoxia, arising from respiratory problems, heart-brain connection abnormalities, and other factors, can lead to EEG slowing in COVID-19.
  • Heart Rate Variability: The section examines the role of HRV in COVID-19, suggesting that changes in HRV could indicate autonomic dysfunction and contribute to hypoxia and cognitive symptoms.
  • Burst Suppression: It describes the burst suppression EEG pattern observed in some COVID-19 patients and its association with reduced brain metabolism.
  • Overlap with Sleep Apnea: The section notes that EEG patterns in COVID-19 patients with hypoxia may resemble those seen in sleep apnea, suggesting potential shared mechanisms.

Strengths

  • Exploration of multiple mechanisms
