Overall Summary

Overview

This systematic review and meta-analysis investigated the effects of various exercise training (ExTr) programs on blood lipid levels, aiming to determine the magnitude of change, optimal exercise type, and sufficiency of existing data. Researchers analyzed 148 randomized controlled trials (RCTs) comprising 8673 participants, comparing aerobic, resistance, and combined training interventions to control groups. The study employed meta-analysis and trial sequence analysis (TSA) to synthesize data and assess the robustness of findings. Results showed modest but significant improvements in all five major lipid markers (total cholesterol (TC), high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL), low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL), triglycerides (TGD), and very low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (VLDL)), with combined training exhibiting the greatest benefit. The study's comprehensive approach and use of TSA provide strong evidence supporting exercise as a valuable tool in dyslipidemia management.

Key Findings

  • Exercise training significantly improved all five major lipid markers. Meta-analysis revealed modest reductions in TC (3.5%), LDL (7.5%), TGD (9.9%), and VLDL (11.7%), and a modest increase in HDL (6.3%). These findings were derived from comparing pre- and post-intervention lipid levels in exercise and control groups across 148 RCTs. This is important because even small improvements in lipid profiles can contribute to reducing cardiovascular disease risk. The improvements may potentially allow for lower medication doses or delay medication initiation.
  • Combined aerobic and resistance training (CT) emerged as the most effective exercise modality for improving the lipid profile. This finding is based on sub-group analyses comparing the effects of different exercise types. CT likely exerts its superior effect by combining the calorie expenditure benefits of aerobic exercise with the increased basal metabolism associated with resistance training, leading to more favorable changes in lipid metabolism. This has practical implications for exercise prescription, suggesting that CT should be prioritized for individuals with dyslipidemia.
  • Trial sequence analysis (TSA) confirmed the statistical significance and futility of the findings. TSA, a statistical method, determines if the accumulated data is sufficient to draw firm conclusions. Reaching futility means further studies are unlikely to alter the conclusions regarding exercise's positive effects on lipid outcomes. This finding strengthens the evidence base and reduces the need for additional, potentially redundant research on this topic.
  • Meta-regression analyses revealed relationships between specific exercise variables and lipid changes. For example, greater aerobic exercise frequency and duration were associated with larger reductions in TC, while longer aerobic session times predicted greater increases in HDL. Meta-regression models were used to explore these associations, providing insights into how exercise prescription can be optimized for maximal lipid benefits. This information can be used to tailor exercise programs to individual needs and preferences.
  • While average effects were positive, prediction intervals (PIs) indicated substantial variability in individual responses to exercise. PIs, which estimate the range of likely effects in future studies, were relatively wide, suggesting that exercise may not be equally effective for everyone. This variability highlights the complexity of lipid metabolism and the potential influence of individual factors such as genetics, diet, and other lifestyle habits.

Strengths

  • The study's comprehensive approach, including a systematic search across multiple databases and analysis of a large number of RCTs (148 trials, 8673 participants), enhances the reliability and generalizability of the findings. The large sample size and diverse range of studies included contribute to the robustness of the meta-analysis, increasing confidence in the results.
  • The use of TSA strengthens the study significantly. TSA helps to control for both Type I (false positive) and Type II (false negative) errors, particularly relevant in meta-analyses due to the cumulative nature of evidence. This increases the confidence that the observed effects are real and not due to chance or insufficient data.
  • The inclusion of meta-regression analyses adds depth to the findings by exploring the relationships between exercise variables and lipid changes. This approach moves beyond simple comparisons of exercise versus control groups and provides valuable insights into how specific exercise parameters can be optimized to maximize benefits for lipid management.

Areas for Improvement

  • The abstract and discussion could more clearly quantify the clinical significance of the "modest" effect sizes observed. While statistical significance is important, the practical implications for CVD risk reduction need further elaboration. Future research could calculate the number needed to treat (NNT) to prevent one CVD event based on the observed lipid changes, or relate the changes to established CVD risk reduction thresholds. This would provide more concrete information for clinicians and patients.
  • The study acknowledges the generally poor quality of included studies based on TESTEX scores. Future research should prioritize higher-quality RCTs with detailed reporting of exercise interventions and lipid outcomes. Furthermore, a sensitivity analysis could be performed to assess the impact of study quality on the pooled results. This would help determine if the findings are robust despite the quality limitations.

Conclusion

This systematic review and meta-analysis, the most comprehensive to date, confirms that exercise training leads to small but statistically significant improvements in all five major lipid markers. The achievement of statistical futility through TSA strengthens these conclusions and suggests that further research is unlikely to change them. Combined aerobic and resistance training is the most effective modality, offering practical guidance for exercise prescription. While the observed lipid changes are modest, they may contribute meaningfully to primary CVD prevention. Future research should focus on quantifying the clinical significance of these changes, addressing the limitations posed by the generally poor quality of existing studies, and developing more specific exercise recommendations tailored to individual dyslipidemia profiles, considering factors like age, sex, and other health conditions. This would enhance the translation of these findings into effective, personalized exercise interventions for managing dyslipidemia and reducing cardiovascular disease risk.

Abstract

Overview

This systematic review and meta-analysis investigated the effects of different exercise training (ExTr) types on blood lipid levels. Researchers analyzed 148 randomized controlled trials, finding that exercise training led to modest but significant improvements in total cholesterol (TC), high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL), low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL), triglycerides (TGD), and very low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (VLDL). Combined aerobic and resistance training (CT) proved most effective for managing dyslipidemia. Trial sequence analysis confirmed the findings' statistical significance, suggesting exercise training may be a valuable tool in managing dyslipidemia and potentially reducing the need for medication in primary prevention.

Key Aspects

  • Exercise Training Improves Lipid Profile: All five major lipid markers (TC, HDL, LDL, TGD, and VLDL) showed statistically significant improvements after exercise training, though the effect sizes were modest (3.5-11.7%). This is important because dyslipidemia is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease.
  • Combined Training is Optimal: Combined aerobic and resistance training (CT) was found to be the most effective exercise modality for improving the lipid profile. This suggests that incorporating both types of exercise may offer the greatest benefit for managing dyslipidemia.
  • Trial Sequence Analysis Confirms Futility: The study used trial sequence analysis (TSA) to determine if the existing data were sufficient to draw conclusions. TSA confirmed that the information size was adequate to declare statistical futility, meaning further studies are unlikely to change the conclusions regarding the positive impact of exercise on lipid outcomes.
  • Modest Effects May Impact Medication Needs: While the improvements in lipid levels were modest, they may be clinically relevant, potentially allowing for lower doses of dyslipidemia medication or delaying the need for medication altogether in primary prevention.

Strengths

  • Comprehensive Analysis

    The study included a large number of RCTs and participants, making it a robust and comprehensive analysis of the effects of exercise on blood lipids. This strengthens the reliability and generalizability of the findings.

  • Use of Trial Sequence Analysis

Introduction

Overview

Dyslipidemia is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease (CVD), and its management guidelines have evolved. While medication is crucial, lifestyle changes like diet and exercise are also important, especially for sub-clinical populations. This review focuses on the impact of exercise training (ExTr) on blood lipids. Previous research suggests exercise can modestly improve some lipid markers, but the findings are not always consistent and may be influenced by factors like exercise type, intensity, and energy expenditure. This review aims to provide a contemporary and comprehensive analysis of the effects of different ExTr types on dyslipidemia, including determining the expected changes in lipid markers, clarifying the impact on LDL, TGD, and VLDL, and using trial sequence analysis to assess the sufficiency of existing data.

Key Aspects

  • Dyslipidemia and CVD Risk: Dyslipidemia, characterized by abnormal blood lipid levels, is a primary risk factor for CVD. This underscores the importance of effective management strategies.
  • Evolving Management Guidelines: Dyslipidemia management guidelines have shifted from fixed lipid thresholds to more flexible targets considering co-morbid conditions and individual risk factors. This highlights the need for personalized approaches.
  • Role of Lifestyle Changes: Lifestyle interventions, including diet and exercise, are recommended as part of dyslipidemia management, particularly in sub-clinical populations. This emphasizes the importance of non-pharmacological approaches.
  • Exercise and Lipid Profile: Previous research suggests exercise can improve certain lipid markers, but the effects are often modest and vary depending on the type and intensity of exercise. This points to the need for a more comprehensive understanding of exercise's impact on different lipid fractions.
  • Need for Contemporary Synthesis: Existing literature lacks a comprehensive, statistically robust analysis of the effects of different exercise training types on dyslipidemia. This justifies the current review's aim to provide a more definitive and up-to-date synthesis of the evidence.

Strengths

  • Clear Context and Rationale

    The introduction effectively establishes the context of dyslipidemia as a CVD risk factor and the evolving nature of its management. It clearly explains the rationale for the review by highlighting gaps in the existing literature.

    "While medication remains the cornerstone to DS management, in sub-clinical populations lifestyle change may be initially required [5]. The two lifestyle therapies are dietary management and physical activity (PA)." (Page 2)
  • Specific Aims and Objectives

    The introduction clearly outlines the specific aims of the review, including identifying expected changes in lipid markers, clarifying the impact on specific lipids, and using TSA. This provides a clear roadmap for the reader.

    "The aims of this work were to (i) identify the expected size of change in primary DS markers following different types of ExTr; (ii) clarify if ExTr reduces LDL, TGD and VLDL; (iii) conduct a trial sequential analysis to control the risks of Type I and II errors, and to estimate if the required information size had been reached." (Page 2)

Suggestions for Improvement

  • Expand on the Significance of TSA

    While TSA is mentioned, its importance could be further emphasized. Briefly explaining how TSA strengthens the review and addresses limitations of previous research would be beneficial.

    "conduct a trial sequential analysis to control the risks of Type I and II errors, and to estimate if the required information size had been reached." (Page 2)

    Rationale: This would highlight the methodological rigor of the review and its contribution to the field.

    Implementation: Add a sentence or two explaining the benefits of using TSA, such as reducing the risk of spurious findings due to random error and providing a more robust estimate of the true effect size.

  • Provide a Concise Definition of Futility

    The term "futility" is used but not explicitly defined. Providing a brief, accessible definition would improve clarity for readers unfamiliar with the concept.

    "It remains unclear if the minimum information size exists." (Page 2)

    Rationale: This would ensure all readers understand the key concept of futility and its implications for the review.

    Implementation: Include a concise definition of futility, such as "Futility refers to the point at which further research is unlikely to change the conclusions of a study due to sufficient existing data."

Methods

Overview

This section details the methodology used in the systematic review and meta-analysis of exercise training's effects on blood lipids. The researchers systematically searched databases like PubMed, Web of Science, and the Cochrane Library for relevant randomized controlled trials (RCTs). They included studies that compared exercise training interventions (aerobic, resistance, or combined) to a control group and reported changes in TC, HDL, LDL, TGD, or VLDL levels. Data extracted from the included studies were analyzed using meta-analysis techniques in STATA V.18, including a random-effects model and trial sequence analysis (TSA) to assess the sufficiency of the data and control for errors. Meta-regression was used to explore the relationship between exercise training variables and lipid changes. The study quality was assessed using the TESTEX scale, and risk of bias was evaluated using the ROB 2.0 tool.

Key Aspects

  • Systematic Search Strategy: A comprehensive search strategy was employed across multiple databases to identify all relevant RCTs. This is crucial for minimizing selection bias and ensuring the review captures a wide range of studies.
  • Eligibility Criteria: Specific inclusion and exclusion criteria were defined for study selection, focusing on adult human RCTs comparing exercise training to a control group and reporting changes in blood lipid levels. This ensures the included studies are appropriate for addressing the research question.
  • Data Extraction and Analysis: Standardized data extraction procedures were used to collect relevant information from each study. Meta-analysis techniques, including a random-effects model and TSA, were employed to analyze the pooled data. This ensures a rigorous and statistically sound approach to data synthesis.
  • Trial Sequence Analysis (TSA): TSA was used to control for Type I and II errors and determine if the information size was sufficient to reach futility. This strengthens the conclusions drawn from the meta-analysis.
  • Study Quality and Risk of Bias Assessment: The TESTEX scale and ROB 2.0 tool were used to assess the quality of included studies and the risk of bias. This helps to evaluate the reliability and validity of the findings.

Strengths

  • Comprehensive Search Strategy

    The search strategy included multiple databases and manual searches of reference lists, maximizing the chances of identifying all relevant studies. This reduces the risk of publication bias and strengthens the review's comprehensiveness.

    "We conducted a systematic literature search in PubMed, Web of Science and the Cochrane Library of Controlled Trials up until 30 September, 2023." (Page 2)
  • Use of Trial Sequence Analysis

    Employing TSA is a significant strength, as it helps control for errors associated with repetitive testing in meta-analyses and provides a robust assessment of the sufficiency of the data.

    "To control the risks of Type I and II errors, random errors associated with repetitive testing when updating reviews, and to estimate required information size we used methods and TSA software of the Clinical Trials Unit (CTU), Copenhagen, DK [25]." (Page 4)

Suggestions for Improvement

  • Provide More Detail on Meta-Regression Variables

    The Methods section mentions meta-regression but doesn't specify all the variables included in the models. Listing these variables would enhance transparency and allow readers to better understand the analysis.

    Rationale: This would improve the clarity and reproducibility of the analysis.

    Implementation: List all variables considered in the meta-regression models, including both exercise-related variables (e.g., frequency, intensity, duration) and participant characteristics (e.g., age, sex, baseline lipid levels).

Results

Overview

This section presents the findings of the systematic review and meta-analysis on the effects of exercise training on blood lipids. The analysis included 148 randomized controlled trials (RCTs) with a total of 8673 participants. The results showed that exercise training led to modest but statistically significant reductions in total cholesterol (TC), low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL), triglycerides (TGD), and very low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (VLDL), and a significant increase in high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL). Trial sequence analysis (TSA) confirmed that the available data were sufficient to support these findings. Further analyses explored the effects of different exercise types (aerobic, resistance, combined) and study quality on lipid changes.

Key Aspects

  • Significant Lipid Changes with Exercise: Exercise training resulted in statistically significant improvements in all five primary lipid outcomes: TC, LDL, TGD, VLDL, and HDL. This is a key finding as it supports the beneficial role of exercise in managing dyslipidemia.
  • Modest Effect Sizes: While statistically significant, the improvements in lipid levels were modest, ranging from 3.5% to 11.7%. This highlights that exercise training alone may not be sufficient to achieve large changes in lipid profiles.
  • Combined Training Most Effective: Combined aerobic and resistance training (CT) was found to be the most effective modality for improving the lipid profile. This suggests that incorporating both types of exercise may offer the greatest benefit.
  • Trial Sequence Analysis Supports Findings: TSA confirmed that the information size was adequate to reach futility for all five lipid outcomes. This strengthens the conclusion that exercise training has a beneficial effect on blood lipids.
  • Prediction Intervals Show Variability: While the mean effects were favorable, prediction intervals revealed that a substantial proportion of studies did not show beneficial effects for TC, HDL, LDL, and TGD. This highlights the variability in individual responses to exercise training.

Strengths

  • Clear Presentation of Results

    The results are presented in a clear and organized manner, with key findings summarized effectively. This makes it easy for readers to understand the main outcomes of the analysis.

    "Total cholesterol (TC) MD – 5.90 mg/dL (95% confidence interval (CI) – 8.14, – 3.65), high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL) 2.11 (95% CI 1.43, 2.79), low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL) – 7.22 (95% CI – 9.08, – 5.35), triglycerides – 8.01 (95% CI – 10.45, – 5.58) and very low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (VLDL) – 3.85 (95% CI – 5.49, – 2.22) all showed significant but modest 3.5–11.7%, improvements following ExTr." (Page 4)
  • Inclusion of Trial Sequence Analysis

    The use of TSA strengthens the study by confirming the sufficiency of the data and controlling for errors associated with multiple testing. This increases confidence in the reported findings.

    "TSA indicated all analyses exceeded minimum information size to reach futility." (Page 4)

  • Provide More Visualizations

Discussion

Overview

This is the most comprehensive analysis of exercise training for dyslipidemia management to date, demonstrating statistical futility for all five lipid outcome measures using trial sequence analysis (TSA). Exercise training showed modest but favorable benefits (3.5-11.7%) across 148 randomized controlled trials (RCTs). These changes may contribute to primary cardiovascular disease prevention and potentially reduce or delay medication needs. Combined training (CT) was found to be the optimal exercise type. However, the study quality was generally poor, with most studies having low TESTEX scores.

Key Aspects

  • Statistical Futility Achieved: This study is the first to demonstrate statistical futility for all five lipid outcomes (TC, HDL, LDL, TGD, VLDL) using TSA. This means further research is unlikely to change the conclusion that exercise improves these markers.
  • Modest but Clinically Relevant Benefits: While the effect sizes were modest (3.5-11.7%), they may still be clinically relevant for primary CVD prevention, potentially reducing or delaying medication needs.
  • Combined Training Optimal: Combined aerobic and resistance training (CT) was found to be the most effective for managing dyslipidemia, likely due to the combined benefits of calorie expenditure from aerobic exercise and increased basal metabolism from resistance training.
  • Poor Study Quality: The majority of included studies had low TESTEX scores, indicating poor quality and reporting. This raises concerns about potential bias and limits the strength of the evidence.
  • Specific Exercise Recommendations: The discussion provides recommendations for exercise programming based on the type of dyslipidemia, suggesting tailored approaches may be necessary.

Strengths

  • Comprehensive and Novel Analysis

    The study is the most comprehensive to date and the first to demonstrate statistical futility for all five lipid outcomes using TSA. This strengthens the conclusions and provides a valuable contribution to the field.

    "This is the most comprehensive pooled analysis on ExTr for DS management to date and the first to demonstrate statistical futility for all five outcome measures, utilizing TSA analyses." (Page 8)
  • Clear and Concise Summary of Findings

    The discussion effectively summarizes the key findings, including the modest benefits, the optimal exercise type, and the limitations related to study quality. This makes it easy for readers to understand the main takeaways.

    "Our work produced clear recommendations on the optimal exercise prescription for DS management. Data from 148 RCTs with sufficient information size demonstrated favourable, modest benefits ranging from 3.5% to 11.7%." (Page 8)

Suggestions for Improvement

  • Further Discussion of Clinical Significance

    While the discussion mentions the potential clinical relevance of the modest benefits, it could be strengthened by providing more specific examples or quantifying the potential impact on CVD risk reduction. This would make the findings more impactful for clinicians.

    "Changes of 3.5–11.7% following ExTr will offer primary cardiovascular disease (CVD) prevention that may delay DS medication commencement, or reduce dosage." (Page 8)

    Rationale: This would enhance the practical implications of the findings and provide more concrete guidance for clinical practice.

    Implementation: Provide specific examples of how the observed changes in lipid levels might translate into reduced CVD risk, or estimate the number needed to treat (NNT) to prevent one CVD event based on the observed effect sizes.

  • Address the Limitations of Poor Study Quality

    The discussion acknowledges the poor study quality but doesn't fully explore its potential impact on the findings. Discussing the potential biases introduced by low TESTEX scores and how these might affect the interpretation of the results would strengthen the analysis.

    "The quality and reporting of published studies on exercise training and lipid management was generally poor, with most studies yielding a TESTEX score < 10." (Page 8)

    Rationale: This would enhance the transparency and rigor of the analysis by acknowledging and addressing potential limitations.

    Implementation: Discuss the specific types of bias that might be introduced by low TESTEX scores (e.g., selection bias, performance bias, detection bias) and how these biases could potentially influence the observed effect sizes. Consider conducting sensitivity analyses to assess the robustness of the findings to variations in study quality.

Conclusions

Overview

Exercise training leads to small but positive changes in lipid profiles. There's enough data to confidently say exercise improves lipid levels. Combined training (aerobic and resistance) seems to be the most effective. These lipid changes may help prevent cardiovascular disease, regardless of whether someone is also taking medication. Different types of dyslipidemia might require slight adjustments to the exercise program.

Key Aspects

  • Favorable Lipid Changes with Exercise Training: Exercise training has a positive impact on lipid profiles, although the changes are small. This is important because even small changes can contribute to overall cardiovascular health.
  • Sufficient Data for Conclusion: The study has gathered enough data to confidently conclude that exercise training improves lipid levels. This means further research is unlikely to change this conclusion.
  • Combined Training is Optimal: Combining aerobic and resistance training is the most effective approach for improving lipid profiles. This suggests that a balanced exercise program is beneficial.
  • Relevance for Primary CVD Prevention: The changes in lipid levels from exercise training can help prevent cardiovascular disease, even if someone is also on medication. This highlights the importance of exercise as a preventative measure.
  • Tailored Exercise for Different Dyslipidemias: Different types of dyslipidemia may require specific adjustments to the exercise program. This suggests a personalized approach to exercise prescription is beneficial.
